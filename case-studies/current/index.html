The Problem

A calendar isn't just a place to put your meetings. It's how you protect what matters most to you: your time, growth, goals.

2.1 billion people rely on digital calendars to plan their lives, and Google Calendar is the most used of them — with 500M+ active users. But for all that reach, it was never built to hold the things people are working toward: a job search, a marathon, a new language.

Daily task-management lacks organization, and long-term progress has no home.

The Solution

How might we help users translate their daily tasks and long-term goals into a realistic, task-unified weekly schedule?

1. Long-term Goal Planning

Set how often, how long, and which days, and your goal turns into real sessions on your calendar. Check them off as you go, and track progress with a running bar and subtasks that carry over from one session to the next.

Recurring Session Generation

You set the frequency, length, and customizations once. Then, the system generates your individual calendar sessions.

Goal planning screens

Persistent Progress Tracking

The progress bar measures your completion and subtasks carry over between goal sessions, so each check-in builds on the last.

Progress bar & subtasks

2. Native Task-Tracking & Organization

A drag-and-drop Kanban board (Planned / In Progress / Done) that stays in sync with a sidebar list, so you can organize your work from whichever view fits the moment.

Kanban ↔ Sidebar Sync

Moving a task on the board updates its status in the sidebar automatically, and changes in the sidebar reflect back on the board. Both views stay accurate no matter which one you're working from.

Kanban board & sidebar

Categories, Priority & Filters

Star your priorities, sort tasks into categories, and filter the list down to what actually matters right now.

Categories & filters

Both live entirely inside Google Calendar's existing visual language: same popups, same sidebar accordion pattern, same Create flow — so the learning curve is zero.

Process

Market Landscape

A SWOT made the gap obvious: Google Calendar's strength is reach and integration, but its weakness is structural — no categorization, no project or goal scaffolding for multi-week work. Tools like Jira, Notion, and Todoist solve organization, but none of them live where the time actually gets booked.

Existing Tools:

  • Strong task & project organization
  • Goal scaffolding for multi-week work

Our Opportunity:

  • Long-term goal tracking
  • Natively inside the calendar people already use
  • Zero learning curve — familiar GCal patterns

Sizing the Opportunity

TAM of 3B adults with access to tech narrows to a SAM of 2.1B digital calendar users, with an initial SOM of 420M (20% of that base) — students and early-career professionals juggling school, work, and personal growth at once.

TAM / SAM / SOM diagram

Measuring Success

KPIs track the full funnel — installs (Acquisition), % of users who set a goal within 48 hours (Activation), DAU/WAU (Engagement), 30/90-day retention, and Chrome Web Store rating (Satisfaction). Three 90-day OKRs anchor the bigger picture:

1

Drive adoption in target communities

2

Build trust through consistent value

3

Encourage proactive planning habits, not just one-time installs

Takeaways

The hardest constraint was also the best one: build entirely inside an interface 500 million people already trust, using only the patterns that already exist there. That discipline is what made the feature feel native instead of bolted on — every popup, every sidebar accordion, every recurring-event mechanic was already familiar, which meant the only new thing a user had to learn was a habit, not an interface.

A current doesn't just push you, it carries you. It's natural, it's powerful, and it's invisible — until you look back and see how far you've moved.

What's Next

We're currently in the process of submitting Current to Google's Chrome Web Store review for publishing. From there: usability testing on the goal-creation flow with real installs, carrying out the GTM and marketing plan to drive adoption, tracking KPIs against our OKRs to validate real usage, custom and completion-based sorting for tasks, and exploring how the same model could extend beyond Chrome to other calendar platforms.
